Clinical Researcher to Software Developer [Blog]

Hello! Welcome to my career transition journey! I am a Certified Clinical Research Coordinator (CCRC/ACRP) and Certified Health Education Specialist (CHES/NCHEC). In 2008 I moved from VA to NC for a Social/Clinical Research Specialist postion working on an obesity prevention study called "Way to Health."

Several years later I secured another Social/Clinical Research Specialist postion this time in a Radiation Oncology department within an academic medical center where I coordinated numerous research studies with individuals undergoing radiation and/or chemotherapy for cancer treatment. Eventually though I felt stagnant in this position as there was no room for professional growth.

After 5 years I left that position in 2017 for a new one, this time in a different aspect of clinical research but unfortunately it just wasn't a good fit. The universe and my own misery were both telling me it was time for a CHANGE. I'd heard about Momentum, a computer coding/programming bootcamp in Raleigh-Durham, NC and decided to check it out. It peaked my interest! I had experience in creating and maintaining websites in my previous postions and had always really enjoyed it.

After doing a lot of research on how coding skills might complement and enhance my clinical reasarch skills and the prospect of employment in the tech field, I really started to consider transitioning my career to tech. With Momentum's great community outreach via social media, I attended a local Meetup crash-course in coding in June 2018. Yep, still interested!

Now, keep in mind, I've been interviewing for several new positions during this time. Although these jobs will provide paycheck and a some project managment and team management experience, the learning curve is short and the professional ceiling very low.

Knowing the committments required to attend the full bootcamp program of 3 months (financial and sans paycheck), I'm still not ready to make a commitment. Thankfully Momentum offers a 2-week Coding Foundations course prior to the start of the full-immersive program in a few weeks. A perfect fit! And so, this blog begins........

Day Three


Teaching improvements:
  • Don't spend too much time answering advanced questions from those who have more than beginner experience; 60 seconds or less is more than adequate. It may make others feels less competent and uses class time that doesn't benefit everyone.
  • Hands-on walk-throughs of lab/homework are more efficient and beneficial than trouble-shooting one student's particular problem. This would serve several purposes:
    1. Keeps everyone on the same page.
    2. Allows each students to learn where they made mistakes.
    3. Reviews and solidifies the material for those who understood it initially.
    4. Reduces the langugage barrier, both the techincal language for newbies and those with English as a non-native language.
  • Don't use multiple shortscuts when writing example code. Write code like a beginner would, individually, stet-by-step.
  • Defintely provide AMPLE lab time when introducing Git, GitHub, and CMD line.
  • Just watching an instructor live code is not effective. It would be better to present the concept then have a working, hands-on example in class for students to code i.e. responsive design lecture.
